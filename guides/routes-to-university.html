Routes from Apprenticeship to University

The different pathways from apprenticeship qualifications to undergraduate and postgraduate study.

Route 1: Apply to University Through UCAS (or Directly)

Most universities accept applications from apprenticeship completers. Your apprenticeship qualification — particularly at Level 3 or above — can count towards entry requirements, often alongside or instead of A-levels.

What matters most is demonstrating that you have the academic foundation and motivation for the course. Many admissions teams are actively looking to diversify their intake, and an apprentice who's spent two years in a working lab has a story that stands out.

Tips:

  • Contact the admissions team directly before applying. Explain your background — many universities have widening participation or contextual admissions teams who can advise you.
  • Ask whether your apprenticeship maps to their entry requirements. Some universities have specific policies for this; others assess case by case.
  • UCAS has a tariff points system, but not all apprenticeships carry tariff points. Don't let that put you off — your experience and qualification still count.

Route 2: Top Up to a Full Degree

If you've completed a Level 4 or 5 apprenticeship (equivalent to HNC or HND/foundation degree), some universities offer "top-up" programmes. These are typically one year of full-time study (or longer part-time) that convert your existing qualification into a full BSc or BA.

This can be a very efficient route if you already have a higher apprenticeship under your belt.

Route 3: Degree Apprenticeship (Level 6)

If you want a full degree but prefer to keep working and earning, a degree apprenticeship might be the right fit. These take three to six years and result in a full bachelor's degree. Your employer funds the training through the Apprenticeship Levy, so there are no tuition fees for you.

The catch: you need an employer who's willing to support you through it, and places can be competitive. But if you're already working in science and your employer is levy-paying, it's worth having the conversation.

Route 4: Postgraduate Study (Master's and PhD)

This is the route fewer people talk about — but it's absolutely possible. If you have a Level 6 qualification (a degree, whether from a degree apprenticeship or via another route), you're eligible to apply for master's programmes and, ultimately, PhD studentships.

We've written a whole separate guide on PhD funding (see below) because it deserves proper attention. But the headline is: there is nothing in the rules that says you can't do a PhD after an apprenticeship. People have done it. People are doing it right now.

What About Recognition of Prior Learning (RPL)?

Some universities will give you credit for learning you've already done during your apprenticeship, which means you might not have to repeat modules that cover ground you've already covered professionally. This is sometimes called Accreditation of Prior Learning (APL) or Recognition of Prior Experiential Learning (RPEL).

It's not automatic — you'll usually need to put together a portfolio or have your prior learning assessed — but it can shave time off a degree programme and it's always worth asking about.

Won't I Be "Behind" Everyone Else?

No. You'll be different from everyone else — and that's an advantage. You'll have professional experience, laboratory skills, workplace maturity, and a much clearer sense of why you're studying than most 18-year-olds arriving at university for the first time.

Lecturers notice this. Supervisors value it. And when it comes to applying for PhD positions or industry roles later, having "real world" science experience from day one is something many graduates wish they had.
