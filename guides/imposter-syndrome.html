Guide 4

You Belong Here

Why imposter syndrome hits harder when you didn't take the "normal" route — and what to do about it.

Let's Name It

You're sitting in a lecture theatre, or a lab meeting, or a PhD interview, and a voice in your head says: "You don't belong here. You're going to be found out. Everyone else went to a proper university and did proper A-levels and knows things you don't. You're a fraud."

This is imposter syndrome. It's the persistent feeling that you're about to be exposed as undeserving of your position, despite clear evidence that you've earned your place. Research suggests that around two in five university students experience it, and it hits hardest during transitions — exactly the moments when you're stepping up from an apprenticeship into higher education or research.

Here's the thing: imposter syndrome isn't a personal failing. It's a predictable response to being in an environment that wasn't designed with you in mind. When every piece of signage, every assumption, every casual question ("which uni did you go to?") reflects a path you didn't take, it's natural to feel like you're in the wrong place.

You're not.

Why It Hits Apprentices Harder

There are specific reasons why people from apprenticeship backgrounds can experience imposter syndrome more intensely than their peers.

The language gap. Academia has its own vocabulary, its own shorthand, its own in-jokes. If you haven't spent three years at university, you haven't absorbed this language by osmosis. When someone casually references "their dissertation supervisor" or "their viva," and you don't know what they mean, it can feel like proof that you don't belong. It's not. It's just vocabulary, and you can learn it. (That's partly why we wrote the Jargon Buster.)

The network gap. Traditional students arrive at university or a PhD programme with academic references, lecturer relationships, and a cohort of peers who all came through the same pipeline. You might not have any of that. Building an academic network from scratch takes time, and until it exists, you can feel isolated.

The cultural gap. Academic culture has unwritten rules about how to speak in seminars, how to email professors, how to present at conferences. These aren't hard to learn, but nobody teaches them explicitly. When you break one accidentally — using a first name when you should have said "Professor," or not knowing the etiquette of a journal club — it can feel mortifying. It shouldn't. Everyone makes these mistakes. You just notice yours more.

The narrative gap. The story society tells about who becomes a scientist is depressingly narrow: bright child → good school → top university → research career. If your story doesn't match that template, you can internalise the idea that you're an exception who slipped through the net, rather than someone who got here through a different but equally valid route.

What Actually Helps

Talk about it. This is the single most effective thing you can do. Tell someone — a friend, a colleague, a mentor, a counsellor — how you're feeling. You will almost certainly discover that they feel it too. Imposter syndrome thrives in silence and withers in daylight.

Keep a record of your achievements. Not a boastful CV — just a private list. Every time you solve a problem, get positive feedback, pass an assessment, present a finding, or do something you didn't think you could do, write it down. When the voice in your head says "you don't deserve to be here," open the list. The evidence tells a different story.

Separate "not knowing something" from "not belonging." You will encounter things you don't know. That's not evidence of fraud — it's the entire point of education. Nobody arrives at university knowing everything. The difference is that when someone from a traditional background doesn't know something, they think "I need to learn this." When someone from a non-traditional background doesn't know the same thing, they think "I shouldn't be here." Challenge that thought. It's doing you no favours.

Find your people. Seek out other non-traditional students, mature students, first-generation students. Most universities have societies, support groups, or widening participation communities where you'll find people who understand your experience. ARC exists partly for this reason — to build a community of people who get it.

Use your difference as a lens. Your background gives you a perspective that most academics don't have. You know what science looks like in practice, in the workplace, at the bench. You can bridge the gap between theory and application in ways that are genuinely valuable. That's not a deficit — it's a superpower.

A Final Thought

The fact that you're even reading this — that you're considering university, or a master's, or a PhD — means you're the kind of person who doesn't stop at "good enough." You've already done something most people haven't: entered a career in science through a route that required grit, independence, and resilience.

The voice that says you don't belong is wrong. You earned your place. Now take it.
