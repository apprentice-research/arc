Guide 11

Telling Your Employer You Want More

How to talk to your manager about further study — whether you want their support or just their blessing.

Why This Is Hard

Leaving a job you got through an apprenticeship can feel disloyal, especially if your employer invested heavily in your training. You might worry about letting people down, burning bridges, or being seen as ungrateful.

These feelings are understandable, and they're almost always unfounded. Good employers want their people to grow — even if that growth takes them elsewhere. And many apprentice employers are genuinely proud when their former apprentices go on to further study. It validates the quality of the training they provide.

But the conversation still needs handling well.

Before You Say Anything

Get your ducks in a row first. Don't have the conversation until you know what you want to do and roughly when. "I'm thinking about maybe possibly doing a PhD sometime" gives your employer nothing to work with. "I'm planning to apply for funded PhD positions starting next October" gives them a timeline and clarity.

Check your contract. Some apprenticeship or employment contracts include clauses about staying with the employer for a certain period after completing your apprenticeship, or about repaying training costs if you leave early. Know what you've signed before you have the conversation. Most of these clauses are standard and manageable, but you don't want surprises.

Consider whether you want support or just notice. There's a big difference between "I'm leaving to do a PhD" and "I'd love your support in pursuing a PhD." Some employers may be willing to provide a reference, fund a part-time master's, allow you study leave, or even co-fund a PhD as an industry partner (CASE studentships work this way). Others will simply wish you well. Knowing what you're asking for helps you frame the conversation.

How to Have the Conversation

Talk to your direct manager first. Not HR, not the CEO, not your colleagues in the break room. Your line manager should hear it from you, privately, before anyone else.

Frame it positively. Lead with gratitude and growth, not dissatisfaction. Something like: "I've really valued my time here, and the apprenticeship has given me skills and experience I wouldn't have got anywhere else. It's actually because of that experience that I've become interested in going further — I want to pursue postgraduate research in [area]."

Be specific about timing. "I'm planning to apply for positions starting next October, so I'd be looking to leave around [date]." Employers appreciate notice. The more time you give them, the more goodwill you preserve.

Ask for what you need. If you'd like a reference, ask. If you'd like them to be a professional referee on your PhD application, explain what that involves. If you'd like time off to attend an interview or open day, ask for that too. Most reasonable employers will say yes.

Be prepared for a range of reactions. Your manager might be delighted and immediately supportive. They might be surprised and need a few days to process. They might be disappointed to lose you. All of these are normal. The key is to be honest, professional, and firm in your plans while being respectful of their reaction.

What If They Want You to Stay?

Your employer might offer you incentives to stay: a promotion, a pay rise, more interesting projects. These are worth considering — genuinely. But be honest with yourself about whether they address the underlying motivation. If what you want is the depth, freedom, and qualification that a PhD provides, a pay rise won't scratch that itch.

It's also worth noting that some employers can be genuine partners in your PhD journey. Industrial CASE studentships involve a company co-funding and co-supervising a PhD alongside a university. If your employer has research questions they'd benefit from having investigated, and they have the budget to contribute, this could be a way to pursue a PhD while maintaining the relationship. Raise this possibility if it feels appropriate — but only if it genuinely interests you. Don't pursue a CASE studentship solely to avoid an awkward conversation.

After the Conversation

Keep being excellent at your job. Don't check out the moment you've announced your plans. The way you leave is how people remember you.

Stay in touch. Your former employer is part of your professional network. They may be a collaborator, a referee, a source of samples or data, or simply someone who cheers you on from the sidelines. Don't underestimate the value of maintaining the relationship.

Don't feel guilty. You were an apprentice, not an indentured servant. Your employer benefited from your work just as you benefited from their training. Moving on to further study is not betrayal — it's progression. That's what the word "career" means.
