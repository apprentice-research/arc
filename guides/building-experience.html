Guide 10

Getting Research Experience Before You Apply

Practical ways to bridge the gap between your apprenticeship and a PhD application.

Why Research Experience Matters

PhD admissions panels want evidence that you can do research — not just that you're interested in it. They want to see that you've encountered the process of asking questions, designing approaches, analysing data, and drawing conclusions. You don't need publications. You don't even need formal academic research experience. But you do need to show that you've engaged with the process in some way.

The challenge for apprentices is that your work, while scientifically rigorous, may not have been framed as "research" in the traditional sense. Part of building your profile is learning to recognise and articulate the research elements of what you've already done, and finding ways to extend them.

Recognise What You Already Have

Before looking for new experience, take stock of what you've got. During your apprenticeship, you may have:

Designed or optimised a protocol. That's method development — a core research skill.

Investigated why something wasn't working. That's troubleshooting, which involves hypothesis testing and systematic investigation.

Collected and analysed data. Whether it was quality control data, patient samples, or experimental measurements, you've practised data collection, interpretation, and potentially statistical analysis.

Written reports or presented findings. Scientific communication is a key research skill, even if your audience was your manager rather than a journal editor.

Contributed to a project with a specific scientific question. Even if you weren't the one who framed the question, you participated in answering it.

Write all of this down. Frame it in research language. This becomes the raw material for your CV and personal statement.

Ways to Build More

Ask for research-adjacent tasks at work. If you're still employed in a scientific role, talk to your line manager about taking on a small investigative project. Could you analyse an existing dataset? Write up a case study? Design and run a small experiment to address a recurring problem? Many employers are supportive of staff development, especially if it also benefits the organisation.

Volunteer in a university research lab. This can range from a few hours a week to a more substantial placement. Contact research groups at local universities whose work aligns with your interests and explain your background. Some groups welcome motivated volunteers, especially those with practical lab skills. Even a few months of exposure to an academic lab environment will give you insight into how research works and a potential referee for your PhD application.

Apply for research internships or placements. Some organisations offer short-term research placements specifically aimed at people considering further study. The Wellcome Trust, NIHR, and individual universities sometimes run these. They're competitive but worth looking for. UKRI and some research councils occasionally fund short research experiences too.

Attend conferences and seminars. Even as an observer, attending scientific conferences gives you exposure to how research is communicated, what current questions look like in your field, and who the active researchers are. Many conferences offer reduced or free registration for early-career attendees. University departmental seminars are usually open and free.

Take on a science outreach role. While not research per se, volunteering as a STEM Ambassador or participating in public engagement activities demonstrates enthusiasm, communication skills, and involvement in the scientific community. It also connects you with academics and researchers.

Do an open-access online course. Platforms like FutureLearn, Coursera, and edX offer courses in research methods, statistics, bioinformatics, and specific scientific topics — often from leading UK universities. These won't replace hands-on experience, but they can fill specific knowledge gaps and demonstrate initiative.

Making It Count

Whatever experience you build, document it. Keep notes about what you did, what you learned, and what questions it raised. When the time comes to write your personal statement or talk in an interview, you'll want to draw on specific examples and reflections, not vague memories.

And frame your experience through the lens of research skills, not job titles. "I worked as a lab technician" is a job description. "I independently optimised a qPCR protocol, troubleshot contamination issues using systematic controls, and presented my findings to the project team" is evidence of research capability.
