Finding Your Supervisor: The Unwritten Rules

How to find the right academic, what to say when you email them, and what to do when they don't reply.

Why This Matters

In the UK, especially in the sciences, your PhD supervisor is the single most important factor in your doctoral experience. They'll shape your project, mentor your development, write your references, and influence your career for years. Choosing well matters enormously — and so does making a good first impression.

If you've been in academia, you probably already know people. You've had lecturers, dissertation supervisors, lab group contacts. You can ask around. But if you're coming from an apprenticeship, you may not know a single academic. That's fine. This guide walks you through the whole process, from finding someone whose work excites you to sending that first nerve-wracking email.

Step 1: Figure Out What You're Interested In

Before you start searching for supervisors, spend some time clarifying what area of research excites you. You don't need a fully formed research question. But you do need to be able to say something more specific than "I'm interested in biology."

Think about the work you've done during your apprenticeship. What problems did you find most interesting? What questions came up that you wished you could explore further? What techniques did you enjoy most?

Write down three or four themes or questions that genuinely interest you. These don't have to be perfect — they're a starting point for your search.

Step 2: Find People Working in Your Area

There are several ways to find potential supervisors:

University department websites. Go to the department pages of universities you're interested in. Most will have staff profiles listing each academic's research interests, current projects, and recent publications. This is the most reliable way to get a sense of someone's work.

FindAPhD.com. If you're looking at advertised PhD projects, the supervisor's name will be listed. This is a good way to discover academics you hadn't heard of.

Google Scholar. If you've read a paper or textbook chapter that fascinated you, search for the author on Google Scholar. You can see their other publications, who they collaborate with, and how active they are. This is a powerful way to go from "I liked this paper" to "this is the person I want to work with."

Your existing network. Do you know anyone who's done a PhD, works in research, or has academic contacts? Ask them. Even a tenuous connection can open a door. If your apprenticeship training provider had links with a university, that's worth exploring too.

Conferences and seminars. Many universities run free public seminars. Attending these — even online — can introduce you to researchers and their current work. It also gives you something specific to mention when you make contact.

Step 3: Do Your Homework

Before you email anyone, spend serious time understanding their work. You don't need to become an expert, but you should:

Read at least two or three of their recent papers (or at the very minimum, the abstracts and introductions). Look at their research group's website if they have one. Understand what their current projects are about. Check whether they have funded PhD positions advertised.

This isn't optional. Academics receive a lot of generic enquiries, and the ones that get responses are the ones that show genuine engagement with the work.

Step 4: Write the Email

This is the part that feels terrifying. Here's how to make it less so.

Subject line: Be specific and professional. Something like "PhD enquiry — [your area of interest]" works well. Avoid vague subjects like "Question" or "PhD."

Salutation: Use "Dear Professor [Surname]" or "Dear Dr [Surname]." Check their actual title on the university website. Getting this right signals that you've done basic research.

Opening paragraph: Introduce yourself briefly. Your name, your current role, and one sentence on your background. "My name is [X], and I'm a laboratory scientist with three years of experience in [field], having completed a Level 6 apprenticeship at [employer]." Keep it short.

Middle paragraph: Explain why you're contacting them specifically. Reference their work — ideally something recent and specific. "I was particularly interested in your 2024 paper on [topic], and the way your group approaches [aspect]. This connects closely to questions I've encountered in my own work, particularly around [your interest]." This shows you've read their research and thought about how your interests align. It's the most important part of the email.

Closing paragraph: State what you're looking for. "I'm exploring PhD opportunities starting in [year] and would welcome the chance to discuss whether there might be a fit with your research group. I'd be happy to share my CV if that would be useful." End with "Yours sincerely" and your full name.

Length: The whole email should fit comfortably on one screen. Three to four short paragraphs maximum. Academics are busy. Respect their time.

What not to do:

Don't send a mass email that's obviously generic. If you can replace the recipient's name with anyone else's and the email still makes sense, it's too generic.

Don't attach your CV unsolicited unless they've asked for it or you've seen instructions saying to include one. Offer to send it instead.

Don't be overly deferential. You're a professional making a professional enquiry. Be respectful, but you don't need to grovel.

Don't lie or exaggerate. If you've only read the abstract of their paper, don't claim to have studied it in depth. Academics can spot this immediately.

Step 5: What Happens Next

If they reply positively: Brilliant. They might suggest a video call, ask for your CV, or point you towards a specific project. Follow their lead, be prompt, and be prepared.

If they don't reply: Don't panic. Academics get a lot of email, and some are genuinely terrible at replying. Wait two weeks, then send a polite follow-up. One follow-up is fine. Two is the maximum. If you still hear nothing, move on to the next person on your list.

If they say they don't have capacity: This isn't a rejection of you. It means they don't have funding, or they've already filled their positions, or they're on sabbatical. Thank them, and ask if they can recommend anyone else in the field. Academics often know who has funding, and a warm referral is gold.

A Note on Confidence

If you're reading this and thinking "but why would a professor want to hear from me?" — that's a completely understandable feeling, and it's wrong. Supervisors want motivated, capable students who are genuinely interested in their research. Your background is unusual, yes, but unusual is interesting. You've done real science, in real settings, with real consequences. Most 21-year-olds applying for PhDs cannot say that.

Send the email.
