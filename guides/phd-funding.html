How PhD Funding Works

The practical guide to who pays for a PhD, what a stipend is, and why you won't be in debt.

The Basics: What Does "Funded" Mean?

A funded PhD studentship typically covers two things: your tuition fees (so you pay nothing to the university) and a tax-free stipend — essentially a living allowance paid to you throughout your studies. For the 2025–26 academic year, the minimum UKRI stipend is £20,780 per year, with a higher rate of around £22,780 if you're studying in London.

It's not a fortune, but it's a liveable income — and it's tax-free, which helps. Some studentships offer more than the minimum, and there are often additional pots available for research costs, conference travel, and training.

Who Funds PhDs?

The biggest public funder is UK Research and Innovation (UKRI), which is an umbrella body covering nine research councils. The one most relevant to life sciences and health research is the Biotechnology and Biological Sciences Research Council (BBSRC), but there's also MRC (Medical Research Council), EPSRC (Engineering and Physical Sciences), NERC (Natural Environment), and others depending on your field.

UKRI doesn't fund students directly. Instead, it gives block grants to universities through Doctoral Training Partnerships (DTPs) and Centres for Doctoral Training (CDTs). The university then advertises studentships and selects candidates.

Other major funders include:

  • NIHR (National Institute for Health and Care Research) — particularly relevant for applied health research and clinical sciences
  • Wellcome Trust — one of the world's largest biomedical research charities
  • Cancer Research UK, British Heart Foundation, and other disease-specific charities
  • Individual universities — many offer their own internally funded studentships
  • Industry-funded studentships — where a company co-funds your research, often with a CASE (Collaborative Awards in Science and Engineering) partnership

How Do I Find Funded PhD Positions?

The best places to look are:

  • FindAPhD.com — the UK's main PhD vacancy listing site. You can filter by subject, funding status, and location.
  • jobs.ac.uk — academic job listings, including funded studentships
  • University department websites — many advertise studentships on their own pages before they appear elsewhere
  • Directly contacting academics — if you've read someone's research and it excites you, email them. Seriously. Many supervisors are happy to hear from motivated candidates and may know about funding opportunities that haven't been advertised yet.

Do I Need a Master's First?

Not always. Many PhD programmes in the UK accept applicants with a strong bachelor's degree (typically a first or upper second class, i.e., 2:1 or above). Some DTPs and CDTs include a master's year as the first year of an integrated "1+3" programme, so you'd do both in one funded package.

That said, a master's can strengthen your application — especially if your undergraduate route was non-traditional. It gives you time to develop research skills and build a relationship with a potential supervisor.

Will They Take Me Seriously With an Apprenticeship Background?

This is the fear, and it's understandable. But here's the truth: PhD admissions in science are primarily about your ability to do research. If you can demonstrate scientific thinking, laboratory competence, curiosity, and motivation, your background is an asset, not a liability.

Many DTPs now explicitly value diverse pathways and non-traditional backgrounds. The research councils have been pushing for widening participation in doctoral training, and an applicant who's come through an apprenticeship brings a perspective that enriches any research group.

What matters most is the quality of your application: a clear sense of why you want to do research, evidence that you can think scientifically, and ideally a connection with a supervisor whose work interests you. Your apprenticeship gave you lab skills, problem-solving ability, and professional resilience — all things that make for a good PhD student.

Key Deadlines to Know

Most funded PhD studentships for an October start are advertised between November and February, with application deadlines typically in January to March. Some rolling opportunities exist year-round, but the biggest funding rounds follow this cycle. Start looking early — and don't be afraid to make contact with potential supervisors before the formal application window opens.
