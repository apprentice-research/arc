Guide 8

How to Read a Scientific Paper

It's a skill, not a talent. Here's how to learn it.

Why This Matters

If you're thinking about postgraduate study or a PhD, you're going to need to read scientific papers. Not textbooks, not Wikipedia, not news articles about science — actual peer-reviewed journal articles. And the first time you try, it will probably feel impossible.

This is not because you're not smart enough. It's because nobody teaches you how to do it. Scientific papers are written by specialists for specialists, using dense language and assuming a lot of background knowledge. Even experienced researchers find papers outside their immediate field hard to read.

The good news: reading papers is a learnable skill. It gets dramatically easier with practice. And once you can do it, you've unlocked the primary source material of science — the place where new knowledge actually lives.

The Anatomy of a Paper

Most scientific papers follow the same structure, often called IMRAD: Introduction, Methods, Results, and Discussion. Understanding what each section is for — and what to get from it — makes the whole thing much more manageable.

Title and Abstract. The title tells you what the paper is about. The abstract (usually 150–300 words at the very beginning) gives you a compressed summary of the whole thing: why they did it, how they did it, what they found, and what it means. The abstract is your first filter. If it's not relevant or interesting to you, move on.

Introduction. This sets the scene. It explains the background to the research question, what's already known, what the gap in knowledge is, and what the authors set out to investigate. This section is usually the most readable part of the paper, because it's written to bring you up to speed.

Methods. This describes exactly how the experiments were carried out — what materials were used, what techniques were employed, how data was collected and analysed. If you've worked in a lab, parts of this will feel familiar. You don't always need to read every detail of the methods on your first pass. Come back to it if you need to understand how a specific result was generated.

Results. This presents the data — usually in the form of graphs, tables, and images, with accompanying text that describes what was found. The figures are often the most informative part of the paper. Learn to read them carefully: look at the axes, the error bars, the sample sizes, the statistical significance markers.

Discussion. This is where the authors interpret their results, explain what they think the findings mean, compare them to previous work, acknowledge limitations, and suggest what should be done next. This section is opinion as well as fact — the authors are making an argument, and it's your job to assess whether the evidence supports it.

References. The list of cited papers at the end. This is incredibly useful — it's a curated reading list that tells you what other papers are relevant to this topic. If you want to go deeper, start here.

How to Actually Read One

Here's the secret that nobody tells you: you don't read a paper from beginning to end like a book. You read it in passes, each time going deeper.

Pass one: the five-minute skim. Read the title, abstract, and the first and last paragraphs of the introduction and discussion. Look at the figures and their captions. At the end of this pass, you should be able to answer: What was the question? What did they find? Why does it matter? If the paper isn't relevant to you, stop here. You've lost five minutes, not five hours.

Pass two: the structured read. Now read the introduction properly to understand the context. Then go through the results section, figure by figure. For each figure, try to understand what it's showing you before reading the accompanying text. Then read the discussion to see how the authors interpret the results. Skip the methods for now unless something in the results confuses you.

Pass three: the deep dive. This is for papers that are central to your work or that you need to understand thoroughly. Now read the methods in detail. Check whether the experimental design supports the conclusions. Look at the statistics. Read the supplementary material. Follow up on key references. This pass might take a couple of hours for a complex paper, and that's normal.

Practical Tips

Keep a vocabulary list. You will encounter unfamiliar terms. Every time you hit one, write it down and look it up. Within a few weeks of regular reading, you'll find the same terms recurring and your speed will increase dramatically.

Start with review articles. A review article summarises the state of knowledge on a topic by synthesising many individual studies. These are much easier to read than primary research papers, and they'll give you the background knowledge you need to tackle original research. Search for "[your topic] review" on Google Scholar.

Use Google Scholar. It's free, it indexes most scientific literature, and it shows you how many times a paper has been cited (a rough indicator of its influence). You can also set up alerts for new papers on topics you care about.

Don't expect to understand everything. Even experienced scientists don't fully understand every paper they read. Understanding the main finding and its significance is a reasonable goal for your first few months of paper reading. The details will come with time.

Read regularly. Like any skill, reading papers improves with practice. Try to read one paper a week related to your area of interest. It will feel painfully slow at first. Within a couple of months, you'll notice a dramatic improvement.

Talk about what you read. Explaining a paper to someone — even if they're not a scientist — forces you to identify what you understood and what you didn't. If you can summarise the main finding in one or two sentences, you understood the paper.

Where to Access Papers

Many journal articles are behind paywalls, but there are several legitimate ways to access them for free:

PubMed Central (for biomedical papers) often has free full-text versions. Google Scholar sometimes links to freely available versions. Many authors post preprints or accepted manuscripts on their personal or institutional websites. Your local university library may offer public access to some databases. If you're enrolled as a student, your university library will give you access to most journals electronically.

If you can't access a paper, it's also completely acceptable to email the corresponding author and ask for a copy. Most scientists are happy to share their work.
