Guide 6

Before You Apply: An Honest Self-Assessment

A PhD is brilliant — but it's not for everyone. Here's how to figure out whether it's right for you.

Why This Guide Exists

Most PhD advice starts from the assumption that you've already decided to do one. This guide starts earlier — at the point where you're curious, maybe a bit excited, but not sure whether it's actually the right move. That uncertainty is healthy. A PhD is a significant commitment of time, energy, and (sometimes) earning potential, and it deserves proper thought.

This isn't here to talk you out of it. It's here to help you make a decision you're confident in.

What a PhD Gives You

A PhD trains you to answer questions that nobody has answered before. That sounds grand, but in practice it means you'll spend three to four years developing deep expertise in a specific area, learning to design experiments, analyse data, think critically, communicate complex ideas, and manage a long-term project with uncertain outcomes.

At the end, you'll have a doctoral qualification — the highest academic degree available. You'll also have a set of transferable skills that are valued across research, industry, policy, consulting, publishing, and many other sectors.

But a PhD also gives you something harder to quantify: the experience of going genuinely deep on something you care about. If you've ever read a paper or encountered a problem at work and thought "I wish I could spend months figuring this out properly," that's the instinct a PhD feeds.

What a PhD Costs

Not money, usually — most science PhDs are fully funded (see our funding guide). But it does cost time, and it costs opportunity.

Three to four years is a significant chunk of your twenties or thirties. During that time, your friends who stayed in industry may be earning more, getting promoted, buying houses. The PhD stipend is liveable but modest — currently around £20,780 per year, or about £22,780 in London. That's likely less than you're earning now if you're in a scientific role.

There's also an emotional cost. PhDs involve extended periods of uncertainty, failure, and self-doubt. Not everyone thrives in that kind of environment. That's not a character flaw — it's a matter of fit.

Questions to Ask Yourself

These aren't trick questions, and there are no right answers. But being honest with yourself now saves a lot of pain later.

Do I actually enjoy research, or do I enjoy the idea of research? There's a difference. Research is mostly not eureka moments. It's troubleshooting, repetition, reading papers you don't understand, and waiting for results. If your favourite parts of your apprenticeship were the investigative, problem-solving, "why does this happen?" moments, that's a good sign. If you mostly enjoyed the structured, task-oriented aspects of the work, a PhD might frustrate you.

Am I driven by curiosity or by the qualification? Both are valid motivations, but they'll sustain you differently. Curiosity will carry you through the inevitable rough patches. If your primary motivation is "I want a Dr in front of my name" or "I think I need a PhD to be taken seriously," those are worth examining. A PhD is an intensely personal endeavour, and external motivations can wear thin by year two.

Can I handle ambiguity? In your apprenticeship, you probably had clear objectives, defined milestones, and regular feedback. A PhD has some of that structure, but much less. There will be months where you don't know if your approach is working. Your supervisor will guide you, but they won't manage you day-to-day. You need to be comfortable with not knowing — and with sitting in that discomfort.

Am I prepared to earn less for a few years? Not forever — PhD graduates generally earn more over their careers. But during the PhD itself, and potentially for a year or two afterwards if you stay in academic postdoc positions, your income will be lower than if you'd stayed in your current role. Is that manageable for your circumstances?

What do I want to do afterwards? You don't need a detailed plan. But it's worth thinking about whether your goal requires a PhD. If you want an academic career in research, a PhD is essential. If you want to work in R&D in industry, a PhD is a strong advantage in many fields. If you want to move into science communication, policy, or management, a PhD can help but isn't always necessary. If you're mainly looking for career progression within your current organisation, there may be faster routes.

Am I excited about a specific area of research? A PhD is three to four years on one topic. If there's a question or a field that you find genuinely fascinating — something you'd read about on your own time, something you bring up in conversation — that's a strong foundation. If you're more generally interested in "doing science" but can't point to a specific area, you might want to spend more time exploring before committing.

A Decision Is Not a Deadline

You don't have to decide right now. You can explore — attend open days, read about potential supervisors' work, talk to current PhD students, apply for some research experience (see our guide on building research experience). The more you learn about what a PhD actually involves, the better your decision will be.

And if you decide it's not for you? That's a perfectly good outcome. A PhD is one route to a fulfilling career in science. It's not the only one.
