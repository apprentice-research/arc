Guide 17

A Guide for Parents, Teachers, and Careers Advisors

Supporting a science apprentice — understanding their options and championing their journey.

Who This Guide Is For

This guide isn't for the apprentice themselves — they have the rest of this website. This is for the parents, partners, family members, teachers, careers advisors, line managers, and mentors who want to understand what's possible and how to help.

You might be reading this because your teenager is considering a science apprenticeship and you're not sure it's the right choice. Or because your apprentice is talking about university and you don't know what that involves. Or because someone you care about has mentioned a PhD and you have no idea what that means in practical terms.

Whatever brought you here, welcome. The fact that you're reading this already puts you ahead of most people in being able to support someone on this path.

First Things First: Apprenticeships Are Real Qualifications

A science apprenticeship is not a consolation prize, a gap year, or a sign that someone wasn't academic enough for university. It is a structured programme that combines on-the-job training with formal study, leading to nationally recognised qualifications.

Apprenticeships are available at multiple levels. A Level 3 apprenticeship (such as Laboratory Technician) is formally equivalent to A-levels. A Level 6 apprenticeship (a degree apprenticeship) is a full bachelor's degree, awarded by a university — the same qualification, with the same letters after your name. The apprentice just earned it while working, instead of while sitting in a lecture theatre.

This matters because the language around apprenticeships often implies they're "less than" traditional routes. They're not. They're different, and the practical experience they provide is a genuine asset — one that many employers and universities increasingly value.

What Are Their Options After an Apprenticeship?

Science apprentices have more options than most people — including many careers advisors — realise.

Continue in their current role and progress through industry. Many apprentices build successful careers in pharmaceutical companies, hospitals, research institutes, and other scientific organisations, advancing through promotion, further training, and professional registration (see below) without further formal study.

Top up to a full degree. If the apprenticeship led to a Level 4 or 5 qualification (such as an HNC or foundation degree), they can complete a full bachelor's degree in just one year through a "top-up" programme. Student finance is available for this.

Go to university as an undergraduate. Apprenticeship qualifications count as valid entry requirements for university. A Level 3 apprenticeship can lead to undergraduate study; a degree apprenticeship leads to postgraduate options.

Do a master's degree. A one-year master's (MSc or MRes) builds research skills and can be a stepping stone to a PhD. Some can be done part-time alongside work.

Do a PhD. A funded doctoral research programme, typically three to four years, leading to a Dr title. Most science PhDs in the UK are fully funded — tuition fees are paid and the student receives a living stipend. A PhD is not something the apprentice (or their family) needs to pay for.

Pursue professional registration. The Science Council offers professional standards — RSciTech, RSci, and CSci — that provide formal recognition of competence and can support career progression without further academic study.

What Is a PhD, and Should They Do One?

A PhD (Doctor of Philosophy) is the highest academic qualification. In science, it involves spending three to four years conducting original research — running experiments, analysing data, and writing a thesis that contributes new knowledge to their field.

A funded PhD in the UK means the student does not pay tuition fees and receives a tax-free stipend (currently around £20,780 per year, or £22,780 in London). This is a living allowance, not a loan or a debt. It does not need to be repaid.

During a PhD, your person will: develop deep expertise in a specific area, gain skills in research design, data analysis, scientific writing, and project management, potentially publish research papers and present at conferences, and earn the right to use the title "Dr."

A PhD is a significant commitment — it involves long hours, emotional ups and downs, and a period of relatively modest income. It's the right choice for someone who is genuinely curious, resilient, and excited about a specific area of science. It's not the right choice for someone doing it because they think they "should" or because they can't think of anything else.

The best thing you can do is support them in making their own decision, ideally after they've read our guides on self-assessment and what a PhD actually involves.

How to Be Supportive (Practical Tips)

Take their ambitions seriously. If your apprentice says they want to do a PhD, don't dismiss it because "nobody in our family has ever done that." First-generation students succeed every day. What they need is encouragement, not a reality check.

Don't panic about money. The most common worry from parents is cost. For PhDs, the answer is almost always: it's funded. They won't need to pay fees, and they'll receive a living stipend. It's less than a salary, but it's liveable. For undergraduate study, student loans cover fees and contribute to living costs, and repayment only begins when they earn above the threshold.

Understand the timeline. Applications for funded PhDs typically open in September–November, with deadlines between December and March, for an October start. If they're planning to apply, they need to be researching and making contact with potential supervisors several months in advance. Master's applications are usually between January and July.

Help them with what you can. You might not know anything about science, but you can proofread a personal statement, help them prepare for an interview, drive them to an open day, or simply listen when they're stressed. Emotional support is just as important as practical help.

Don't compare routes. If they have siblings or friends who went straight to university, avoid comparing timelines. The apprentice route takes longer but builds different (and often stronger) foundations. The fact that they're 25 and starting a PhD while their university friend started at 22 doesn't mean they're behind — it means they took a different path.

Learn enough to ask good questions. You don't need to understand their research. But asking "How's the application going?" or "Have you heard back from that supervisor you emailed?" shows that you're paying attention and that their journey matters to you.

For Careers Advisors Specifically

If you work in a school, college, or careers service, here are the things that would make the biggest difference to science apprentices:

Know the progression routes. Many careers advisors are well-informed about apprenticeships as an entry point, but less aware of what comes after. Degree apprenticeships lead to bachelor's degrees. Bachelor's degrees lead to master's and PhD opportunities. Science apprentices can and do become Dr.

Don't treat "university or apprenticeship" as a permanent fork. The most powerful message you can give a young person is that these routes are not mutually exclusive. An apprenticeship can lead to university. A degree can lead to an apprenticeship-style role. Career paths in science are rarely linear, and the best ones often combine both.

Signpost to ARC. We exist to fill the information gap. If you have students or clients considering science apprenticeships, or current apprentices wondering about their next steps, point them our way. Everything on this site is free.

Challenge the narrative. If you hear colleagues or students say that apprenticeships are "for people who can't get into university," push back. That narrative is factually wrong and actively harmful. Some of the best scientists in the country started as apprentices.

For Teachers and Lecturers

If you teach or supervise science apprentices — whether at a training provider, an FE college, or through an apprenticeship programme — you are often the most influential person in their decision to progress.

Encourage them to think beyond the current role. Many apprentices are so focused on completing their qualification and doing well at work that they don't look up. Help them see the longer view: what could they do in five years? Ten years? What questions excite them beyond their current remit?

Write strong references. If an apprentice asks you for a reference for further study, take it seriously. Speak to their intellectual ability, their curiosity, their capacity for independent work. Don't just confirm that they passed their modules — help the admissions team understand who this person is and what they're capable of.

Connect them to opportunities. If you know of research placements, open days, summer schools, or funded positions, share them. Many apprentices don't have anyone else in their life who would know about these things.

For Employers

If you employ science apprentices and one of them comes to you saying they want to do a PhD, that's not a loss — it's a success. You trained someone well enough that they want to push further. That reflects brilliantly on your organisation.

The best response is to support them: provide a strong reference, allow time for interviews, and explore whether a collaborative arrangement (such as a CASE studentship) might work for both of you. Even if they leave, they'll remember how you responded — and they may come back as a collaborator, a client, or a more senior scientist.

A Final Word

The person you're trying to support has already done something impressive. They entered science through a non-traditional route, which required courage, independence, and a willingness to be different. They've proved that the conventional pathway isn't the only one that works.

Now they're thinking about going further. Your role is simple: believe in them, support them, and get out of their way when they need to fly.
